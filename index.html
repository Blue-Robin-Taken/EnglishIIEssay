The use of Force Against Hamas

Context

World War One

After the collapse of the Ottoman empire, people like Theodore Herzl wanted to create a Jewish state where their ancestors once lived. This is called Zionism. After World War One, Zionists convinced the Brittish and League of nations to place a mandate over the area of Palestine called the Balfour Declaration. However, the mandate brought with it some problems. The local arab population opposed the mandate and Palestinians revolted in the Arab Revolt of 1936-39. The British government shipped thousands of troops while the local Jewish population created a militia called the Haganah (although not allowed by the British).

World War Two

In World War Two, many Jews were escaping Nazi rule because of the Holocaust. However the British government decided to restrict Jewish migration to the mandate because Palestinians rejected a multiple state solution with "Jewish and Arab states" (“Israel - Jewish Homeland, Zionism, 1948 | Britannica”). Yet, after the war the UN decided to create a two state solution in Palestine (a two state solution is one Arab and one Jewish). The Arabs in the region continued to be against the two state solution and revolted again. A nationalist called "Hajj Amin al-Husseini" led Arabs to attack Jews in the mandate which sparked a Zionist group called the "Stern Gang" that broke apart from the "Irgun Zvai Leumi" movement to attack a Palestinian village called "Deir-Yassin" that killed 100 people.

The War of 1948

Israel finally declared independence in May of 1948. However, the Arab population didn't like this at all. In the war of 1948, the surrounding Arab nations attacked Israel (“Milestones in the History of U.S. Foreign Relations - Office of the Historian”). The states of "Egypt, Iraq, Lebanon, Syria and Transjordan" joined that attack to fight Israel. Yet, the through the use of "militias" like the "Stern Gang" and "Irgun Tzvei Le'umi," rivalries between arab states, "conflicting goals," and the first ceasefire that allowed Israelis to recuperate gave the Israelis a victory that gained them "one-fifth more territory than the original partition plan" (“Israel - Armistice, Refugees, Conflict | Britannica”, Aljazeera). This displaced many of the Arab populations but those that stayed were given "full citizenship" (Rekhess).

Gaza (the focus of this website)

After Israel won the war of 1948, the land of Gaza was held by Egypt (not annexed). In 1956 Egypt tried to nationalize the Suez Canal. This is important because the Suez canal provides a major trade route to European powers. So, in retaliation, France and Britian asked Israel to fight with them and take the canal. They successfully defeated Egypt and took the Canal but also held gaza under occupation until "international pressure soon forced Israel to withdraw" (“Gaza | Map, History, & Facts | Britannica”).

Six day war

The six day war was the war that the Arab states of Egypt, Jordan, and Syria all fought against Israel but lost after six days (“Six-Day War | Definition, Causes, History, Summary, Outcomes, & Facts | Britannica”). After the war, Israel had the least amount of casualties and was able to occupy Gaza.

Camp David Accords

Instead of giving Gaza back to Egypt, Israel wanted "some form of Palestinian autonomy" (“Milestones in the History of U.S. Foreign Relations - Office of the Historian”). At the Camp David Israel showed a "formula for Palestinian self-government in Gaza." However, a two state solution wasn't created because "Palestinian representatives refused to participate."
